A crucial tool for digital musicians.

Primer: What's MIDI?

MIDI, which stands for Musical Instrument Digital Interface, is a protocol for communication between digital instruments and things that make sounds.

You can think of it as a way for your digital piano to talk to your computer: when you play a note on your piano keyboard, your computer is told which note was played, and how hard it was pressed. Your computer can then use this information to make a noise. Perhaps a piano noise, but perhaps a guitar, a drum, a synthesizer, or anything else your music-making computer might have installed.

How people use MIDI

One of the nice things about MIDI is that it's a very space-efficient way of communicating complex musical ideas.

For example, a sixty-second audio recording of a piano will occupy a little under 2.5 megabytes of space. Meanwhile, the complete MIDI representation of Carly Rae Jepsen's "Call Me Maybe" occupies a miniscule 53 kilobytes.

Another great benefit of working with MIDI is editing flexibility. After recording a MIDI part, a musician can go back and move notes around, correct timing issues, and adjust the amplitude of particular notes until the result is perfect.

Because of these advantages, it's common for electronic musicians to entirely avoid using recorded audio in their work, instead recording MIDI from a variety of hardware devices (like MIDI drum pads and MIDI keyboards) and generating sounds in their computers instead.

MIDICatch

I made MIDICatch to act as a live backup tool for MIDI-centric music makers.

Because I often experiment with melodies and beats on my MIDI controller before I start recording with my music-making software, I can save only those musical ideas that I'm able to play twice. The problem is that I can't always do this - often, a good idea will come and go before I can play it again. Unless I happen to be recording when inspiration visits, the idea is lost forever.

That's why MIDICatch has become incredibly useful to me. Even if the software I use to record MIDI isn't running, MIDICatch faithfully sits in the background, capturing every MIDI note that comes into my computer. If I find myself playing something cool that doesn't get recorded in the normal way, I can just pop open the MIDICatch dropdown in my menu bar and grab the latest recording. It's a truly indispensable tool.
